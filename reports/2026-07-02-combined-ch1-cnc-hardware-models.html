1.1 What is an NC Machine?

📗 CNC Book — Main Text

NC machines are mechatronics products — mechanical systems combined with numerical control electronics. Your PCB drilling machine is a non-cutting NC machine (press/drilling/forming category, distinct from milling/turning).

1.3 Servo Motor Types

📗 DC Servo

Principle: Brush + commutator. Current through armature creates torque.

✅ Pros: Simple control, well-understood math model

❌ Cons: Brush wear, maintenance overhead, being phased out

CNC Use: Older machines only

📗 PMSM — Standard CNC Servo

Principle: Permanent magnet rotor, 3-phase stator. Electronic commutation via FOC (Field-Oriented Control).

FOC uses Clarke transform (UVW → αβ) then Park transform (αβ → dq) to decouple torque and flux — making PMSM behave like a DC motor in the dq-frame.

✅ Pros: No brushes, high torque at zero speed, precise, high power density

⚠️ Cons: Complex control electronics (FOC)

CNC Use: Standard for CNC servo axes — ETEL uses this

🔧 Your Machine
Your ETEL linear motors on X/Y/Z are PMSM-type controlled by FOC inside the ETEL drive firmware. COMET never exposes the FOC internals — they are factory-tuned inside the drive.
📗 Induction AC

Principle: Squirrel cage rotor, induced current. No permanent magnets.

✅ Pros: Rugged, very high speed, low cost

❌ Cons: Less precise position control, speed-dependent torque

CNC Use: Spindle motors (high-speed rotation, not position-critical)

📘 Nise Theory — DC Motor Transfer Function (Nise Ch2 §2.8)

📘 Nise Theory Background

Because FOC transforms PMSM control into the dq-frame (behaves like DC motor), the DC motor transfer function gives you the math model for any servo motor in your machine.

Two coupled physical domains:

⚡ Electrical Domain

  • Armature resistance: $R_a$
  • Armature inductance: $L_a$
  • Back-EMF: $v_b = K_b \cdot \dot{\theta}$

⚙️ Mechanical Domain

  • Rotor inertia: $J$
  • Viscous damping: $D$
  • Motor torque: $T_m = K_t \cdot i_a$

Full transfer function (voltage → shaft angle):

$$\frac{\theta(s)}{E_a(s)} = \frac{K_t}{s \left[ (R_a + L_a s)(Js + D) + K_t K_b \right]}$$

Simplified (with $L_a \approx 0$, since $\tau_e \ll \tau_m$):

$$\frac{\theta(s)}{E_a(s)} = \frac{K_t}{s \left( R_a J s + R_a D + K_t K_b \right)}$$

Second-order system — two poles, one integrator ($\frac{1}{s}$).

Velocity form (what encoder measures):

$$\frac{\Omega(s)}{E_a(s)} = \frac{K_t}{R_a J s + R_a D + K_t K_b} \qquad \tau = \frac{R_a J}{R_a D + K_t K_b}$$

First-order system. Time constant $\tau$ = how fast motor velocity responds to voltage.

💡 Key Link — Why This Matters for COMET Tuning
When COMET runs system identification (auto-tune), it identifies $J$, $D$, $K_t$ by exciting the motor and fitting this transfer function to the measured response. Then it computes PID gains that place the closed-loop poles where you want them. This is the mathematical foundation of the whole COMET tuning process.
📖 Read the Book — Nise Ch2 §2.8 (pp. 124–132)
Follow the two mesh equations: electrical ($v_a = R_a i_a + L_a \dot{i}_a + v_b$) and mechanical ($T_m = J\ddot{\theta} + D\dot{\theta}$). Take Laplace of both, substitute $T_m(s) = K_t I_a(s)$ and $V_b(s) = K_b s\Theta(s)$, eliminate $I_a(s)$, simplify to final form. Worth doing once by hand.

1.4 Encoder Types

Type Output Position at Power-On Notes
Incremental A/B quadrature pulses, counts from zero Unknown — needs homing Cheaper. Common in older machines.
Absolute Binary position code (e.g. 20-bit EnDat) Known immediately ETEL uses this. No homing needed.
🔧 Your Machine (ACCURET+)

ETEL motors use absolute encoders (EnDat protocol) — position is known at power-on, no homing needed.

Resolution:

$$\text{Theoretical step} = \frac{5\text{ mm}}{10{,}000\text{ pulses/rev}} = 0.5\text{ μm/pulse}$$

Real-world accuracy ±25 μm due to ball screw pitch errors and thermal expansion — the encoder is more precise than the mechanical system.

1.5 Ball Screw and Coupling

📗 CNC Book — Main Text

Ball screw: Converts rotation to linear motion. Lead = mm/revolution. Pre-loaded to eliminate backlash. Resolution = lead / pulses-per-rev.

Rigid Coupling

Accurate, no compliance. Transmits shock to motor bearings.

Flexible Coupling ⚠️

Absorbs vibration. Adds compliance — a spring in the system. Can cause ringing.

Direct Drive

Linear motor, no screw. Highest precision. Most expensive.

📘 Nise Theory — Compliance Adds a Pole

A flexible coupling adds spring $K$ and damper $D$ between motor and load — creating an additional resonant mode:

$$G_{plant}(s) = G_{motor}(s) \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{Ms^2 + Ds + K}}_{G_{coupling}} \cdot G_{load}(s)$$

Where $G_{coupling}$ is a mass-spring-damper (Nise Ch2 §2.5). High $K_p$ excites this resonance → "ringing" in COMET's position error graph.

1.6 CNC Control Loop Configurations

Config Encoder Location Accuracy Stability
Open loop None (stepper) Poor N/A
Semi-closed ← You Motor shaft encoder Good (±25 μm) Easier to tune
Closed loop Table (linear encoder) Highest (±1–5 μm) Harder to tune
Hybrid Motor + table (both) Best of both Most complex
📘 Nise Theory — Loop Config Changes Your Plant

Semi-closed: Feedback measures $\theta_{motor}$. Ball screw converts: $x = \frac{lead}{2\pi} \cdot \theta_{motor}$ — a constant gain. No extra dynamics added. Plant $G(s)$ = motor TF directly.

Full closed: Feedback includes mechanical compliance between motor and table → extra poles, potential resonant pair → harder to stabilize.
